# Return of the Obra Dinn — fate slot locks only on triple-confirm

## Anchor

The book *Catalogue of the Dead* opens to a two-page spread of every crew member as a chalk silhouette. Below each silhouette, three blanks: **Name**, **Fate**, **Killed by / Killer**. The player walks the ship, witnesses death-scenes via the pocket-watch, and writes guesses into the blanks.

The game tells the player *nothing*. Names of senior officers are revealed by their formal addresses in scenes; rank by uniform and crewmate deference; cause-of-death by what's visible (spear, pistol, drowning, illness). When the player commits three guesses to the book and all three are correct, the book *locks* those three entries in ink — a triple-confirm. Until then, every entry is a chalk pencil revision.

The player can identify Henry Brennan as the second mate by hearing "Mr. Brennan, the ladder!" in one scene, *and* observing his coat is dark wool with brass buttons (officer), *and* in another scene the surgeon calls "second mate's not breathing" near his body. Three independent observations triangulated.

## Why this scores high on earned-discovery

Knowledge is forced through the player's mind, not the game's mouth. There is no tooltip, no biography, no "press Tab for character info." The player IS the inference engine; the game is just a witness. Sub-criterion 1 (hidden state not shown directly) hits ceiling — the only way to know is to look. Sub-criterion 3 (diagnostics yield revelation, not data) hits because every confirmed fate re-reads three earlier scenes; the player remembers the boatswain's voice differently after knowing what he survived. Sub-criterion 4 (player describes the citizen) hits because after the book locks, the player can narrate the man's last day from memory.
