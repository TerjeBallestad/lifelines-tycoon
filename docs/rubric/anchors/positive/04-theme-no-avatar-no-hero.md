# Hand-authored — Tiltak description in third-person caseworker voice

## Anchor

A model Tiltak description (planned copy for `int_routine_visit`):

> **Frank schedules Tuesday-morning visits for two weeks.**
>
> Elling's mother is informed in advance. Frank carries a thermos and the same brown folder each week. He does not ask Elling to do anything. After the second visit, Elling sometimes makes the coffee.
>
> *Cost: 1.5h × 2 weeks. Specialist: Frank (mental health worker).*

There is no "you" in the description. No "your caseworker." The verbs ("schedules", "carries", "does not ask") are about Frank's behaviour. The Tiltak narrates a small concrete sequence in the world; the player's only role is to *authorize* it (by paying overskudd + capacity).

## Why this scores high on thematic-coherence

Tiltak copy is the easiest place for theme to leak — most games default to "you cast", "you use", "your ability does X." This anchor demonstrates the discipline: write the Tiltak as if you were a NAV planner describing a delivered service to a colleague. The player legislates and pays; the specialist acts. Sub-criterion 1 lands because the player is grammatically absent from the description. Sub-criterion 2 lands because "schedules", "informed", "carries" are administrative verbs, not game verbs.

## Specific sub-criteria signal

- Sub-criterion 1 (Player role = state, not hero): 3 — zero second-person pronouns. The state's role is the unstated frame; Frank and Elling carry every verb.
- Sub-criterion 2 (Verbs match welfare-state vocabulary): 3 — "schedules", "informed in advance", "carries", "after the second visit": every clause sounds like a session note.
