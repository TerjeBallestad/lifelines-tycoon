# The Roottrees Are Dead — cross-reference is the primary verb

## Anchor

The player is shown a family-tree skeleton: a deceased patriarch, his children, his grandchildren. Each slot has placeholders. The right-hand panel is a 1990s search engine — type "Elias Roottree", get newspaper clippings, magazine covers, yearbook scans, marriage announcements. The player reads, cross-references, and drags names + photos into the tree.

A specific moment: a magazine cover from 1973 shows a woman holding a child captioned *"Elias's pride and joy."* A yearbook from 1971 shows the same woman, captioned "Margaret Holloway, class of '71." A 1972 marriage announcement: "Margaret Holloway weds Henry Roottree." The player triangulates: Margaret Holloway = Margaret Roottree = Henry's wife = mother of the captioned child. The child's identity is still unknown; back to the search engine.

There is no narrator. There is no "press X to investigate." The investigation IS the game.

## Why this scores high on earned-discovery

Each tree slot is locked behind 3–5 independent corroborating documents. The act of *playing* is the act of building the model. Sub-criterion 2 (observations specific to citizen) hits because Margaret is not "a daughter" — she is *the woman in the 1971 yearbook with the dimple, who married Henry, who taught piano lessons in '74.* Sub-criterion 3 (diagnostics yield revelation) hits because finding one new document re-reads every previously-found document; the mental model compounds.

## Specific sub-criteria signal

- Sub-criterion 2 (Observations specific to citizen): 3 — every Roottree is identified by specific corroborating documents; nothing about Margaret could be about Henry's other sister.
- Sub-criterion 3 (Diagnostics yield revelation, not data): 3 — finding the 1971 yearbook re-reads the 1973 magazine; revelation cascades backwards through prior evidence.
