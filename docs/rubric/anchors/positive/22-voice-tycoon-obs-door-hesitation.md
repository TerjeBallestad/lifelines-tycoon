# obs_door_hesitation — physical specificity over emotional label

## Anchor

Verbatim from `docs/superpowers/specs/2026-05-18-economy-prototype-design.md` §9:

> `obs_door_hesitation` — "Elling reaches for the front door, then turns back." → `[trauma:strangers, skill_gap:going_outside]`

The observation describes a physical sequence: reach, turn back. Two verbs. No interior monologue. No "Elling feels afraid"; no "Elling wants to but cannot." The reader supplies the inference; the writer provides only what a NAV report could legitimately claim to have observed.

## Why this scores high on texture-voice

The hard-won discipline is to write what you saw, not what you suspect. "Reaches for the front door, then turns back" is what a caseworker could honestly note in a session record — Frank could have looked through the window and seen exactly this. "Seems afraid", "appears anxious", "is unable to go outside" are interpretations a NAV report would *not* assert without grounds. Sub-criterion 1 (specific over generic) hits because both verbs land on a specific physical action. Sub-criterion 2 (Norwegian-bureaucratic tone) hits because the register is exactly the dry, evidentiary tone of an actual welfare case note.
