# Frostpunk — edicts as the only verb the state has

## Anchor

In Frostpunk, the player has no avatar. The screen shows a frozen crater city; the input is a Book of Laws panel. Each entry — "Extended Shift", "Soup", "Child Labour: Safe Jobs", "Public Penance" — is voted on and then *signed*. After signing, the law applies to every citizen in the city; the player cannot countermand a single Worker. The verb is not "command the worker"; the verb is "make law for everyone." The player never touches a unit.

Lifelines' equivalent verb is **Dispatch Specialist** (planned) or **Try Intervention** (shipped):

- The player chooses Frank-the-mental-health-worker for Tuesday.
- Frank carries out the visit; the player does not control Frank inside the visit.
- The outcome (door opened / door not opened, observations surfaced) is reported back as a case-file entry, not as a controllable scene.

## Why this scores high on thematic-coherence

The Frostpunk model is the strongest existing reference for a no-avatar, no-unit-control state simulator. Lifelines inherits the "the player legislates, others act" stance; this anchor establishes that the player's place in the system is *upstream of every visible character*. Sub-criterion 1 (player role = state, not hero) hits ceiling because nothing in the UI is "you" — Frostpunk shows the city; Lifelines shows the case file. Sub-criterion 2 hits because the verbs are decree-language (sign, edict, ordinance) rather than RPG-language.

## Specific sub-criteria signal

- Sub-criterion 1 (Player role = state, not hero): 3 — Frostpunk has zero avatar; the camera is the state's eye. Lifelines mirrors this with a panel-of-bars HUD, no portrait.
- Sub-criterion 2 (Verbs match welfare-state vocabulary): 3 — "sign", "edict", "dispatch", "review" all land in the bureaucratic register. Compare avoid-list: "command", "summon", "use".
